Internal action equality

What shoud we suggest? Or how should we take action?

1. Addressing gender roles

  • Promoting non-gender biased education in schools, universities as well as efficient and non-stereotyped careers advice services:
    1. Organising open days in companies for girls.
    2. Cooperation programmes with public and/or education authorities to raise awareness on labour markets needs like molengeek.
    3. Participation in career fairs.
  • Making a special effort to attract girls and young women into technical and scientific professions.
    1. Offer the opportunity of an apprenticeships in technical and scientific professions.
    2. Sending women engaged in technical or scientific occupations as “ambassadors” into university or school to inform and raise awareness of girls about opportunities of technical or scientific professions.
  • Promoting the recruitment and retention of women where they are underrepresented.
    1. Reviewing the way in which job titles, job descriptions and advertisements are formulated to enhance their attractiveness for women.
    2. Adapting the workplace to enable a greater gender mix at work (ergonomics, local services).
    3. Informing about internal job offers and career prospects on intranet.
  • Raising awareness of middle managers in companies on ways to promote equal opportunities for all employees.
    1. Highlight top managers’ commitment to a diverse workforce.
    2. Training managers on the benefits of diversity management.
    3. Ensuring good information flow.
    4. Highlighting cases of women breaking new ground.
  • Promoting entrepreneurship as a career option
    1. Identifying potential female business starters and/or top managers, and train them.
  • Encouraging competence development for adults to allow men and women to evolve in their careers throughout life and to address deficits created by the structure of the education system in the past, with particular attention to supporting SMEs in their efforts.
    1. Ensuring transparency and transferability of competences and qualifications.
    2. Promoting the participation of low-skilled workers and/or workers in non-standard employment in further education and training.
    3. Setting gender balance objectives at sectoral level for access to vocational training.

2. Promoting women in decision-making

  • Ensure that the overall working environment supports a more balanced participation of women and men in decision-making.
  • Promoting women in decision-making positions is an investment for a more productive, innovative and stimulating working environment and better economic performance.
  • Key to improve women participation at all levels within enterprises.
    • Promoting competence-based gender neutral recruitment in enterprises
      1. Putting in place transparent and gender neutral recruitment, selection and promotion criteria
      2. Checking that the recruitment of women is proportionate to the number of female applications
    • Retaining women in enterprises, to avoid loss of competence.
      1. Discussing at an early stage with employees their career expectations as well as work-life balance.
      2. Conducting interviews with women leaving a company
      3. Conducting regular staff surveys to check employees satisfaction.
    • Encouraging career development of both women and men
      1. Developing networks, mentoring or self confidence buildin schemes across enterprises.
      2. Setting targets for the promotion of women in proportion to the number of women in the relevant occupational level.
      3. Identifying possibilities for non linear career development paths alternating periods of higher professional involvement and periods of greater family responsibility as an alternative to a culture of unnecessarily long working hours.
      4. Facilitating geographical mobility
    • Promoting female entrepreneurship
      1. Programmes for women willing to start their own business.
      2. Supporting projects to ease generational handover to women in SMEs.
    • Promoting women’s role in the social dialogue at all levels, both on the employers’ side and the trade union side.

3. Supporting work-life balance

  • Successful policies to support work-life balance need to be tailored to the needs of individual women and men, bearing in mind that these can vary throughout the life course and taking into account that long and/or irregular working hours can be an obstacle to reconcile work and family life for both men and women.
  • Ensuring the availability and affordability of quality care facilities, for children, elderly and/or other dependents.
  • Key Elements:
    • Considering flexible working arrangements that can be taken up on a voluntary basis by both women and men.
      1. Flexibility in working time or the organisation of work, such as part-time work, job-sharing, reduced hours, compressed working week, school term-time working, staggered or gliding hours, timeoff and ad hoc home working etc.
      2. Career breaks and parental leave arrangements.
    • Promoting a more balanced take-up of possibilities to ease work-life balance:
      1. Company internal awareness raising schemes to promote take-up of flexible work options
      2. Information campaigns about leave possibilities available to both women and men
      3. Encouraging parents to share leave periods more equally.
    • Increase the availability of accessible and affordable child care facilities and seeking innovative ways of providing essential private household or caring services.
      1. Attracting on site services such as travel office, shops, bank and insurance offices etc.
      2. Sponsoring child care centers or providing an allowance.
      3. Creating funds by collective agreements or other means to “mutualise” the costs of maternity or parental leave allowances. Similar measures for sick children, care outside regular opening hours, etc.

4. Tackling the gender pay gap

  • Informing about existing legislation on equal pay and giving guidance on how to help closing the gender pay gap at different levels.
    1. Practical guide, especially for SMEs, on how to live up to their legal obligations
    2. National or sectoral collective agreements or framework agreements.
  • Developing clear up to date statistics and promoting the availability of gender specific information at the appropriate levels (national, sectoral and/or company level).
    1. have a detailed and analytical picture of the underlying causes of wage differences between women and men.
  • Ensuring that pay systems, including job evaluation schemes, are transparent and gender neutral.
    1. Screening job descriptions to check that they don’t contain discriminatory elements.
    2. Reviewing pay criteria to check that they are not discriminatory.
    3. Developing tool kits comprising different elements in order to give background information to negotiators dealing with gender equality issues and to offer practical advice on what employers and workers can do in enterprises.
    4. Use existing on-line tools providing information on wage formation.
